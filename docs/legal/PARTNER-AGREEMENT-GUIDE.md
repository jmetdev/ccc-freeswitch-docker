# Partner Agreement — Decision Guide

Companion to `PARTNER-AGREEMENT-TEMPLATE.md`. Before sending the template to
counsel (do that — this is not legal advice), decide the business points below.
They drive most of the bracketed placeholders.

## 1. Pick your partner model first

The template is written for a **reseller** model. If you want a different model,
the paper changes significantly:

| Model | Who bills the customer | Who owns the customer contract | Partner compensation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Reseller** (template default) | Partner bills customer; you bill partner | You (via your Customer Agreement/ToS) + partner's own resale terms | Margin between partner price and resale price |
| **Referral / affiliate** | You bill the customer directly | You | Commission (e.g., 10–20% of first-year revenue) |
| **White-label / OEM** | Partner, under its own brand | Partner (backed by your terms to the partner) | Wholesale pricing; heavier legal lift (branding, support, liability flow-down) |

A referral agreement is much shorter and lower-risk — if partners will mostly
just introduce deals, start there instead.

## 2. Key business decisions (map to template sections)

- **Exclusivity (§2)** — Default is non-exclusive. Only grant exclusivity in
  exchange for hard minimum commitments (Exhibit B), and time-box it.
- **Territory (§1.4)** — Geography, market segment, or "worldwide."
- **Support split (§3.3)** — Partner Tier 1 with your Tier 2/3 backing is the
  common reseller pattern; it keeps you out of direct end-user support.
- **Renewals (§3.4)** — The most-fought-over term. Decide whether the partner
  keeps its margin on renewals it doesn't actively work, and who owns the
  renewal conversation.
- **Pricing/discounts (Exhibit A)** — Typical SaaS reseller discounts run
  15–35%, often tiered by volume or certification. Reserve the right to change
  the price list on notice (template uses 30–90 days).
- **Payment risk (§4.3)** — The template makes the partner liable whether or not
  the end customer pays. Keep this; it's the main reason to use resellers.
- **Post-termination customers (§14.4)** — Decide up front: do customers convert
  to direct billing, or does the partner keep margin through the current term?
  Ambiguity here causes disputes.

## 3. Telecom/CPaaS-specific flags (since this product carries voice)

- Confirm whether resale of your service makes the partner (or you) a regulated
  telecommunications provider in each target territory (e.g., FCC filings,
  robocall mitigation/STIR-SHAKEN, E911/emergency calling, number porting,
  lawful intercept). §5(d) puts compliance on the partner, but counsel should
  confirm the allocation is realistic per territory.
- If partners bring their own SIP trunks/carriers, spell out who is responsible
  for call quality, fraud (toll fraud is common), and emergency-call routing.

## 4. What to prepare alongside the agreement

1. **Customer Agreement / Terms of Service** — §3.2 requires every end customer
   to accept your ToS. If you don't have public ToS and a DPA yet, those come
   first; the partner agreement leans on them.
2. **Partner price list** (Exhibit A) and any deal-registration process.
3. **Brand guidelines** referenced in §7.
4. **Partner application/onboarding form** — capture legal entity, tax/resale
   certificates, and banking details.

## 5. How to use the template

1. Fill every `[BRACKETED]` placeholder; delete the option you don't choose
   where the template offers alternatives (§3.3, §14.4, etc.).
2. Delete §14.2 (termination for convenience) if you want partners locked in
   for the term — but most SaaS vendors keep it for themselves at minimum.
3. Send to a lawyer in your governing-law jurisdiction for review, especially:
   resale price language (§4.2), indemnities (§12), liability cap (§13), and
   any consumer/telecom regulatory overlay.
4. Keep the signed version and exhibits under version control or in your CLM
   system; exhibits (pricing) will change more often than the body.
